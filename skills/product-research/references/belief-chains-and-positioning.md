## Necessary Beliefs Framework

Every purchase requires the customer to hold a chain of beliefs. Map these to build messaging that moves prospects through the chain.

### The Belief Chain

```
Unaware → Problem Belief → Solution Belief → Product Belief → Purchase Belief → Action
```

**Problem Belief:** "I have a problem worth solving."
- Requires: awareness of pain, dissatisfaction with status quo
- Messaging: agitate the problem, make it vivid and urgent

**Solution Belief:** "A solution like this exists and could work for me."
- Requires: awareness of the solution category
- Messaging: educate on the approach, normalize the solution

**Product Belief:** "This specific product is the best solution for me."
- Requires: differentiation, proof, trust
- Messaging: unique mechanism, social proof, demonstrations

**Purchase Belief:** "Now is the right time, and this offer is right."
- Requires: urgency, value perception, risk removal
- Messaging: offer framing, guarantees, scarcity (if real)

### How to Map Beliefs for a Product

1. **Start from purchase and work backward:** What must someone believe RIGHT BEFORE they buy?
2. **Identify blockers:** For each belief, what prevents someone from adopting it?
3. **Find proof:** What evidence supports each belief?
4. **Sequence content:** Each piece of content should move someone one belief forward.

### Application to Research

In the persona positioning file, map the complete belief chain for each persona. In the messaging file, ensure ad angles address specific belief transitions. In the objection kill sheet, link each objection to the belief it blocks.

## Levels of Customer Awareness

Eugene Schwartz's 5 levels. Determines messaging approach and ad strategy.

### Level 1: Unaware
Customer doesn't know they have a problem.

**Messaging strategy:**
- Lead with story, curiosity, or entertainment
- Never mention the product directly
- Use content marketing, viral hooks, pattern interrupts
- Example: "I was embarrassed when my friend pointed out..."

**Ad format:** Story ads, curiosity hooks, "did you know" content

### Level 2: Problem-Aware
Customer knows the problem but not that solutions exist.

**Messaging strategy:**
- Agitate the problem
- Validate their frustration
- Begin hinting at a solution category
- Example: "Tired of [problem]? You're not alone..."

**Ad format:** Problem-agitation ads, "relatable" content, before/after setups

### Level 3: Solution-Aware
Customer knows solutions exist but hasn't found the right one.

**Messaging strategy:**
- Differentiate your approach
- Introduce the unique mechanism
- Explain why other solutions fall short
- Example: "Most [products] fail because they [common flaw]. That's why we..."

**Ad format:** Comparison content, unique mechanism reveals, "why this is different"

### Level 4: Product-Aware
Customer knows your product but hasn't bought yet.

**Messaging strategy:**
- Overcome specific objections
- Stack proof and social proof
- Address risk with guarantees
- Example: "Over 10,000 customers have switched — here's why"

**Ad format:** Testimonial compilations, objection-handling content, demos

### Level 5: Most Aware
Customer knows and wants the product — just needs the right offer.

**Messaging strategy:**
- Lead with the offer
- Create urgency (real, not manufactured)
- Make buying frictionless
- Example: "30% off this weekend only — free shipping included"

**Ad format:** Direct offers, retargeting, abandoned cart sequences

### Application to Research

Classify each persona by awareness level. Design ad angles for each level. Ensure the objection kill sheet addresses Level 4 objections specifically (these are the "almost bought but didn't" people).

## Market Sophistication Stages

Eugene Schwartz's market sophistication model. Determines HOW to present your product claim.

### Stage 1: Be First
You're the first to make the claim. Simply state it directly.
- "Lose 10 pounds in 30 days"
- Works when the market hasn't heard this promise before

### Stage 2: Enlarge the Claim
Competitors are making similar claims. Make yours bigger.
- "Lose 20 pounds in 30 days"
- Works briefly but creates an arms race

### Stage 3: Introduce the Mechanism
Claims are commoditized. Explain WHY/HOW your product works.
- "Lose 10 pounds in 30 days with our patented thermogenic blend"
- The unique mechanism becomes the differentiator

### Stage 4: Enlarge the Mechanism
Competitors have mechanisms too. Make yours more impressive.
- "Lose 10 pounds in 30 days with our triple-action thermogenic blend backed by 3 clinical studies"
- Stack proof on the mechanism

### Stage 5: Identify with the Prospect
Mechanisms are commoditized. Connect on identity and values.
- "For women over 40 who've tried everything — finally, a program that gets your body"
- Lead with who they are, not what the product does

### Application to Research

Determine the market's current sophistication stage. This directly shapes:
- How to position the unique mechanism
- Whether to lead with claim, mechanism, or identity
- What competitor messaging reveals about market stage

## Unique Mechanism Framework

Every successful product needs a unique mechanism — the proprietary reason WHY it works differently.

### UMP (Unique Mechanism Proposition)

The WHAT — the specific mechanism, process, ingredient, technology, or approach that makes this product different.

**Template:** "{Product} uses {mechanism} to {outcome} unlike {alternatives} which {limitation}."

**Requirements:**
- Specific and nameable (ideally proprietary/branded)
- Explainable in one sentence
- Tied to a real product feature or process
- Differentiated from competitor mechanisms

### UMS (Unique Mechanism Story)

The WHY — the narrative that explains the mechanism's origin and why it matters.

**Components:**
1. **Discovery:** How was this mechanism found/developed?
2. **Science/Logic:** Why does it work?
3. **Proof:** What evidence shows it works?
4. **Contrast:** Why don't competitors use this approach?

**Template narrative:**
"Most {products} try to {common approach}. But research shows that {insight}. That's why {brand} developed {mechanism} — which {how it works differently}. The result: {outcome with proof}."

### Finding the Unique Mechanism

During research, look for:
1. **Proprietary processes** the brand uses
2. **Ingredient or material differences**
3. **Design innovations** visible in the product
4. **Method or approach** differences (how it's used)
5. **Origin story** (where materials come from, who designed it)

If no clear mechanism exists, help identify one from the product's actual features.

## Offer Architecture

Design the offer structure to maximize conversion and AOV.

### Three-Tier Strategy

| Tier | Purpose | Price Psychology | Contents |
|------|---------|-----------------|----------|
| **Entry** | Lower barrier, acquire customers | "Try it for just..." | Starter size, single item, trial |
| **Core** | Primary revenue driver | "Best value" (anchor to premium) | Standard offering, bundle savings |
| **Premium** | AOV maximizer, anchoring | Makes core look reasonable | Full set, added bonuses, VIP |

### Offer Stack Components

1. **Core product** — the main item
2. **Bonuses** — added value (guides, accessories, extras)
3. **Guarantee** — risk reversal
4. **Urgency** — real time constraints
5. **Social proof** — testimonial integration

### Pricing Psychology
- **Charm pricing:** $47 vs $50
- **Anchoring:** Show premium first, then core seems like a deal
- **Bundle savings:** "Save $X when you buy the set"
- **Per-unit pricing:** Makes bundles look more attractive
- **Subscription:** "Subscribe & save" for consumables

## Discovery Story Framework

A narrative framework for presenting the product's unique mechanism as a story.

### Structure

1. **The Problem** — Vivid description of the frustration
2. **The Failed Solutions** — What the market offers and why it falls short
3. **The Discovery** — How the mechanism was found/developed (curiosity gap)
4. **The Mechanism** — What it is and how it works
5. **The Proof** — Evidence it works (data, testimonials, demonstrations)
6. **The Offer** — How to get it

### Application

Use this framework to:
- Structure long-form sales pages
- Create video ad scripts
- Build email sequences
- Develop founder/brand story content

## Funnel Architecture Mapping

Map research findings to specific funnel stages.

### TOFU (Top of Funnel) — Awareness
**Goal:** Attract attention, create problem awareness
**Content types:** Social posts, short videos, blog posts, viral content
**Research inputs:** Market trends, problem statements, curiosity hooks
**Awareness levels served:** Unaware, Problem-Aware

### MOFU (Middle of Funnel) — Consideration
**Goal:** Educate, differentiate, build trust
**Content types:** Comparison guides, long-form content, webinars, email sequences
**Research inputs:** Competitor analysis, unique mechanism, VoC analysis
**Awareness levels served:** Solution-Aware, Product-Aware

### BOFU (Bottom of Funnel) — Decision
**Goal:** Convert, overcome final objections
**Content types:** Landing pages, retargeting ads, testimonial compilations, offers
**Research inputs:** Objection kill sheet, pricing strategy, social proof
**Awareness levels served:** Product-Aware, Most Aware

### Post-Purchase
**Goal:** Retain, upsell, generate referrals
**Content types:** Onboarding emails, cross-sell offers, review requests
**Research inputs:** Surprise & delight insights, bundle opportunities, persona data

### Application to Research

In the messaging file, tag each ad angle with its funnel position. This helps the organic-content-generator and native-image-ad-generator skills produce content for the right stage.
