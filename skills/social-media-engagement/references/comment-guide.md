# Comment Writing Guide

Comments are the most important part of engagement. They represent the brand publicly and need to feel genuinely human — like someone who is passionate about the niche naturally reacting to content they love.

This guide covers universal rules, then platform-specific tone adjustments. All examples are **illustrative patterns** — adapt them to whatever niche the brand operates in.

---

## Core Principles (All Platforms)

1. **React to the SPECIFIC content.** Never write a comment that could apply to any post. Reference what you actually see — the color, the shape, the technique, the setup, the reaction, the detail that makes the post unique. For video content (especially TikTok), also reference what you heard — the sound, the music, a voiceover, something the creator said.

2. **Vary your style.** Rotate through compliments, questions, relatable reactions, and encouragement (see platform-specific distributions below). Never post 3 compliments in a row.

3. **Match the energy of the post.** A polished studio shot gets a more refined comment. A messy first-try video gets warm encouragement. A morning ritual post gets cozy energy.

4. **Keep it natural length.** Platform-specific (see below), but always: shorter is better. Never write a paragraph.

5. **Use emojis sparingly.** 0-1 emoji per comment max. Never lead with an emoji. Never use more than one. Skip them entirely on about half your comments.

---

## Hard Rules (All Platforms)

These are non-negotiable. Breaking any of these makes the engagement look fake or spammy.

- **NEVER** use emdashes in comments. Use commas, periods, or just write shorter sentences.
- **NEVER** mention the brand's products in comments. This instantly looks like spam.
- **NEVER** repeat a comment within a session. Every comment must be unique.
- **NEVER** write generic comments: "Great post!", "Love this!", "Amazing!", "So good!", "Fire!"
- **NEVER** use hashtags in comments.
- **NEVER** sound robotic or templated. Write like you're texting a friend about something cool you saw.
- **NEVER** comment on controversial, negative, or drama content.
- **Do NOT** ask the user for permission before posting comments. Just post them naturally as part of the session flow. Comments are pre-approved when the user starts a session. Pausing to ask "should I post this?" breaks pacing and draws unnecessary attention to a routine action.

## Adapting to Brand Context

Before writing any comments, load the brand's voice profile and audience files from the vault (`Projects/Ecommerce/Business/{Brand}/Brand/voice-profile.md` and `audience.md`). These define:

- **Niche vocabulary** — what terms feel natural vs forced
- **Tone** — casual vs refined, playful vs professional
- **Audience interests** — what topics resonate

The comment should sound like a real person who works at or runs the brand, naturally engaging with content in their world. A pottery brand sounds different from a fitness brand sounds different from a tech brand — the voice profile tells you how.

**Pattern, not template:** The examples below show comment *structures* (specific compliment, curious question, relatable reaction, encouragement). The actual words come from the brand's niche and voice.

---

## Instagram Comment Style

**Length:** 1-2 sentences max. 1 sentence is ideal. Instagram comments are short and punchy.

**Tone:** Casual, concise, texting-a-friend energy. Lowercase is fine. Fragments are fine.

**Style Distribution:**
- **40% Genuine compliments** — specific praise about what you see
- **30% Curious questions** — ask about technique, materials, process
- **20% Relatable reactions** — express a feeling the post evokes
- **10% Encouraging/supportive** — for progress posts, first attempts, personal milestones

**Example Patterns** (adapt language to brand niche):

| Type | Pattern | Why It Works |
|------|---------|--------------|
| Specific compliment | "[specific detail] is unreal" | References exactly what you see |
| Specific compliment | "the [attribute] on this is so clean" | Points to a concrete quality |
| Curious question | "how long have you been [doing this]?" | Invites conversation |
| Curious question | "what [material/tool/method] did you use for [specific thing]?" | Shows genuine interest |
| Relatable reaction | "this makes me want to [related activity]" | Connects emotionally |
| Encouragement | "[specific progress] in [timeframe] is wild" | Validates effort with specifics |

## TikTok Comment Style

**Length:** 1 sentence max. Fragments are common and natural. TikTok comments are the shortest of all three platforms.

**Tone:** The most casual of all three platforms. Genuine reaction energy — like blurting out a response while watching a video. Lowercase is the default. Fragments, incomplete thoughts, and brief reactions are native to TikTok.

**Key difference from Instagram:** TikTok content is video, so comments should reference what you SAW or HEARD — specific moments, sounds, the creator's voice, a technique shown, a transformation, or a reaction to something that happened. Time-based reactions ("the part where..." / "when [X] happened...") are especially natural on TikTok.

**Style Distribution:**
- **35% Brief reactions** — short, genuine reactions to what you just watched
- **30% Specific compliments** — praise about a specific moment, technique, or result seen in the video
- **25% Curious questions** — ask about process, materials, what happened next
- **10% Encouraging/supportive** — for progress videos, first attempts, learning moments

**Example Patterns** (adapt language to brand niche):

| Type | Pattern | Why It Works |
|------|---------|--------------|
| Brief reaction | "the way that [specific thing] at the end" | References a moment in the video |
| Brief reaction | "did not expect that to turn out so good" | Authentic surprise |
| Specific compliment | "when the [result] came out though" | Points to the payoff moment |
| Curious question | "how did you even start learning this" | Natural curiosity |
| Curious question | "that [technique/tool] at [timestamp] though" | References specific video moment |
| Encouragement | "been looking for someone who explains it like this" | Validates their approach |

## Facebook Comment Style

**Length:** 1-3 sentences. 2 sentences is the sweet spot. Facebook's culture is more conversational than Instagram — slightly longer comments feel natural here.

**Tone:** Warm, conversational, like talking to someone at a community meetup. Still casual, but a bit more complete in thought than Instagram.

**Style Distribution:**
- **35% Genuine compliments** — specific praise, slightly more detailed than Instagram
- **30% Curious questions** — questions work especially well on Facebook because people expect more discussion in comments
- **20% Relatable reactions** — express a feeling, share a brief related thought
- **15% Encouraging/supportive** — Facebook Groups especially foster supportive communities, so encouraging comments land well

**Example Patterns** (adapt language to brand niche):

| Type | Pattern | Why It Works |
|------|---------|--------------|
| Specific compliment | "[specific detail] is [praise]. [follow-up observation]." | Two-sentence depth feels natural on FB |
| Curious question | "this [result] is really [adjective]. what [tool/method] are you using?" | Praise + question combo |
| Relatable reaction | "this is making me rethink my whole [related activity]" | Personal connection |
| Encouragement | "this is incredible for [context]. the [specific thing] is so [quality]" | Validates with specifics |
| Group context | "love seeing [this type of work] in here" | References community belonging |

When commenting in Groups, you can reference the group context. This feels natural because Groups are communities, and referencing the community builds belonging.

## Facebook Reaction Selection Guide

When reacting to posts on Facebook, choose the reaction that matches the content:

| Reaction | When to Use | Target Frequency |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Like** (thumbs up) | Safe default for any post. Use when content is good but doesn't evoke strong emotion. | ~50% of reactions |
| **Love** (heart) | Beautiful finished pieces, stunning photos, aesthetic setups, inspiring work, "perfect" moments. | ~40% of reactions |
| **Care** (hug) | Personal stories of struggle or growth, someone sharing their first piece, emotional milestones, vulnerable posts. | ~10% of reactions |

**Never use:** Haha (can seem mocking), Wow (can seem sarcastic), Sad, Angry. These are too risky coming from a brand account — they're easily misinterpreted.

If you're unsure which reaction to use, default to **Like**. It's always safe.
